# Supercomputer Consulting — Discovery Call Script

## Purpose
This call is for qualification, diagnosis, and next-step alignment.

It is **not** a free consulting session and it is **not** a generic capability presentation.

The objective is to determine:
- Is there a real business issue or decision?
- Is the situation material enough to justify outside advisory support?
- Is Supercomputer Consulting the right fit?
- What is the next sensible step?

---

## Discovery call flow

### Recommended length
- 20 minutes for first call
- 30 minutes if the lead is clearly qualified and more stakeholders are present

### Structure at a glance
1. Opening and frame
2. Context and trigger
3. Stakes and risk
4. Existing work and stakeholders
5. Timing, budget, and decision process
6. Fit summary
7. Close with next step

---

## 1. Opening and frame (2 minutes)
Use a calm, direct setup.

**Suggested language**
"Thanks for making time. I’d like to understand the decision or challenge in front of you, ask a few questions about timing, risk, and scope, and then I can tell you whether I think there’s a fit and what the most sensible next step would be."

If they immediately ask for background, keep it short.

**Short positioning version**
"I work on high-stakes data center decisions where an owner, operator, enterprise team, or investor wants an independent view on risk, resiliency, modernization, diligence, or facility readiness."

---

## 2. Context and trigger (4–5 minutes)
Goal: understand why this conversation is happening now.

### Core questions
- What prompted the conversation right now?
- What decision are you trying to make, or what problem are you trying to solve?
- Is this tied to an acquisition, expansion, modernization effort, reliability concern, or AI / HPC initiative?
- What is the current stage of the project or evaluation?

### Follow-up prompts
- What changed recently?
- Why is this becoming important now?
- Is there a specific milestone or deadline driving this?

### What you are listening for
- Concrete trigger
- Real business urgency
- Defined decision in front of them
- Language that signals pain, risk, or executive attention

---

## 3. Stakes and risk (4–5 minutes)
Goal: surface consequences, uncertainty, and business impact.

### Questions
- What are the biggest risks or unknowns as you see them?
- What concerns leadership most?
- What happens if the decision is delayed, under-scoped, or wrong?
- Where do you think the hidden exposure might be?
- What would be expensive or embarrassing to discover too late?

### Useful probes
- Is the bigger concern capex, reliability, schedule, or operational readiness?
- Are you more worried about technical feasibility or execution quality?
- Is the issue lack of visibility, lack of capacity, or lack of alignment?

### What you are listening for
- Financial impact
- Reliability / uptime exposure
- Executive risk
- Blind spots in assumptions
- Lack of independent validation

---

## 4. Scope, stakeholders, and work already done (4–5 minutes)
Goal: understand the environment and whether there is room for outside value.

### Questions
- What type of facility, portfolio, or environment is involved?
- Who are the main stakeholders?
- Who ultimately signs off?
- What work has already been completed?
- Are internal teams, design firms, vendors, or contractors already involved?
- Where do you feel you still need an independent perspective?

### Useful probes
- What assumptions are currently being accepted without much challenge?
- Where are internal teams most aligned? Where are they least aligned?
- What information do you have today that you trust least?

### What you are listening for
- Complexity
- Decision-maker access
- Existing consultant / vendor landscape
- Whether the prospect actually wants advice or just validation

---

## 5. Timing, budget, and buying process (3–4 minutes)
Goal: establish whether this is a real opportunity.

### Questions
- What timeline are you working against?
- What is the next major decision point?
- Is there an approved budget or a path to one?
- How are you thinking about outside support?
- If this moves forward, who needs to be involved in that decision?

### Useful probes
- Is budget already allocated, or does a business case still need to be made?
- Are you evaluating this as a discrete engagement or a broader advisory relationship?
- What would a successful outside advisor help you achieve in the next 30–60 days?

### What you are listening for
- Real timeline
- Budget signal
- Procurement or approval friction
- Sponsor strength
- Whether the opportunity is active or speculative

---

## 6. Qualification checklist
A lead is typically qualified if most of the following are true:
- Clear decision or project exists
- Timing is within 1–9 months
- Meaningful downside risk exists
- Decision-maker or strong sponsor is involved
- Budget path exists
- Buyer values independent judgment
- Scope fits one of the core advisory offers

### Strong green flags
- Active transaction or expansion
- Reliability issue with real consequences
- Executive sponsor present on call
- Prospect asks precise next-step questions
- They want help framing or validating decisions, not just labor

### Red flags
- They want generic IT consulting
- No project, just vague curiosity
- No budget path, no sponsor, no urgency
- They are fishing for free design work
- They want implementation labor rather than advisory guidance

---

## 7. How to talk about fit

### If the fit is strong
"Based on what you’ve shared, this sounds like a good fit for the kind of work I do. The main issue seems to be [summary], and I think the next sensible step is [scoped follow-up / proposal / document review / stakeholder session]."

### If the fit is possible but needs more clarity
"There may be a fit here, but I’d want to tighten the scope around [timing / stakeholders / technical question / decision process] before I recommend a formal next step."

### If the fit is weak
"I don’t think I’m the best fit for what you’ve described. My work is strongest when the need is independent advisory on high-stakes facility decisions rather than [implementation / general IT / commodity consulting]."

---

## Close and next-step language

### Option 1 — Move to proposal
"The next sensible step would be for me to send a brief scope outline covering objectives, deliverables, timeline, and fee structure. If that works, I can turn that around after I review a bit more context from your side."

### Option 2 — Move to document review
"Before proposing anything broader, the most efficient next step may be a focused review of the existing materials so I can identify the real decision points and any obvious blind spots."

### Option 3 — Schedule stakeholder follow-up
"It sounds like the next useful step is a follow-up conversation with the people who will shape or approve the decision, so we can align on objectives and constraints early."

### Option 4 — Nurture / later timing
"This may be better timed a bit later. I’m happy to send a concise overview and stay available if the project becomes more active or the questions sharpen."

---

## Objection handling

### Objection: “We already have internal experts.”
**Response**  
"That usually makes outside advisory more useful, not less. Internal teams know the environment deeply. The outside value is bringing an independent lens to assumptions, blind spots, and tradeoffs leadership may want challenged."

### Objection: “We already have a design firm / vendor.”
**Response**  
"Understood. My role is different. I’m not there to replace a design team or sell equipment. The value is an owner-side perspective on risk, realism, and downstream consequences."

### Objection: “Can you just send a proposal?”
**Response**  
"I can, but even a short conversation usually makes the proposal substantially sharper. Without context, it’s easy to scope the wrong problem."

### Objection: “Your fee may be high.”
**Response**  
"That may be true relative to lower-cost consulting options. My work is designed for situations where the cost of a poor decision materially outweighs the cost of getting the advisory right."

### Objection: “We’re not ready yet.”
**Response**  
"Fair enough. Often the best time for this work is before the decision hardens. If useful, we can keep it narrow and clarify what should be validated now versus later."

### Objection: “We just need a quick opinion.”
**Response**  
"Happy to have a short conversation first. If the issue is material, though, a quick opinion is usually less useful than a defined review with explicit questions and assumptions."
